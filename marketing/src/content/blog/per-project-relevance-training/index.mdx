# Per-project relevance training

## Working angle

- Core promise: explain that Digest Engine gets better at picking relevant material as the editor uses it, without requiring complicated setup or prompt-writing.
- Audience lens: marketers and newsletter authors who want the system to reflect their judgment, not a generic idea of what is popular.
- Tone: practical, user-friendly, and empowering. This should feel like a feature that adapts to the reader, not one they have to manage constantly.
- Suggested headline direction: *A curation model that remembers what you liked last week.*

## 1. Open with the real frustration

- Start with the mismatch readers already know: most content tools keep serving a mix of obvious stories, loosely related stories, and outright junk.
- Explain that even strong discovery tools often feel generic because they do not truly learn one editor’s definition of relevance.
- Frame the problem in editorial terms: what counts as “worth covering” is different for every newsletter.

## 2. Define per-project relevance training in plain language

- Explain that each Digest Engine project gradually learns what belongs in that specific newsletter.
- Make it simple: when the editor signals “more like this” or “less like this,” the system changes what rises in the shortlist next time.
- Clarify that this learning is scoped to one project, so different newsletters can have different tastes even inside the same organization.
- Avoid technical phrasing like centroids, embeddings, or retraining in the main outline.

## 3. Explain how the feedback loop works

- Introduce the reader-facing mechanism: thumbs up and thumbs down on content.
- Emphasize how lightweight this is: no prompt engineering, no long configuration forms, no need to “teach” the system in a complicated way.
- Show that feedback is a natural extension of the editorial process: the same judgment the writer is already making becomes signal for the next issue.

## 4. Show what changes over time

- Explain that the shortlist gets sharper after a few rounds of use.
- Mention that weak-fit content appears less often, while the kinds of stories the editor consistently values rise faster.
- Make this concrete: after a week or two, the feed should feel more like an editorial assistant and less like a generic discovery engine.
- This section should highlight the cumulative benefit of steady use.

## 5. Give a realistic example

- Walk through a simple example relevant to marketers or newsletter authors.
- Example shape: two newsletters both cover "AI," but one cares about enterprise buying signals and the other cares about model releases and research.
- Show how the same incoming story can be more relevant to one project than the other.
- Use that contrast to explain why project-specific learning matters.

## 6. Explain why this matters for marketers and newsletter authors specifically

- Marketers often need the tool to understand what is useful for their audience, not just what is broadly discussed.
- Newsletter authors need relevance to become more precise over time so they can spend less energy filtering and more energy deciding what to do with the best material.
- Emphasize that the product is learning editorial preference, not just topic keywords.

## 7. Address the likely objection

- Question to address: "Do I need to constantly train this thing for it to work?"
- Answer: no. You start with a reference set and then improve it through normal editorial feedback.
- Explain that routine use is enough to refine the shortlist over time.
- Keep the tone reassuring and practical.

## 8. Make the project-isolation point explicit

- Explain that every project has its own relevance model.
- This matters because one team’s idea of a perfect source list may be another team’s noise.
- Use this section to reinforce that Digest Engine is not trying to force one global content taste across every newsletter.

## 9. Connect the feature to trust and explainability

- Explain that the system is not just guessing blindly. There is a visible reason why certain content rises.
- In user-facing language, point out that the editor can understand that the ranking is shaped by reference content and past feedback.
- Keep this simple and confidence-building rather than technical.

## 10. Close with the takeaway

- Re-state the practical benefit: the more a reader uses Digest Engine, the more the shortlist starts to reflect that project’s editorial instincts.
- Tie this back to outcomes: less filtering, better picks, faster issue planning.
- End with a bridge to adjacent features like authority-aware ranking or draft assembly.

## Notes to preserve from source material

- The key source idea to preserve is that feedback changes what the system sees as a good match over time.
- The promise of "no prompt engineering, just vote" is strong and should remain in some form.
- The project-specific isolation is an important differentiator and should stay prominent.
- The final article should translate explainability into user language rather than low-level relevance math.
