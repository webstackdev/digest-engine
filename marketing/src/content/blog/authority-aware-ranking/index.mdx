# Authority-aware ranking

## Working angle

- Core promise: help readers understand why "authority" is more useful than raw popularity when they are choosing what to cover.
- Audience lens: marketers and newsletter authors who need better judgment, not more noise.
- Tone: practical, editorial, and confident without sounding technical.
- Suggested headline direction: *Trust the editors you already trust.*

## 1. Open with the real problem

- Start with the everyday frustration: most discovery tools reward whatever is loud, viral, or already everywhere.
- Frame the cost in editorial terms: readers do not care that a story was popular, they care that it was worth their time.
- Set up the contrast: popularity measures attention, but authority measures who credible people in your niche are paying attention to.

## 2. Define authority-aware ranking in plain English

- Explain that Digest Engine looks at the people, companies, and topics trusted editors keep linking to.
- Clarify that this is not a generic influencer score or a follower count.
- Emphasize that the system is project-scoped, so authority is based on your niche, not the whole internet.
- Keep the explanation non-technical: the product learns who consistently earns attention from the newsletters and sources you already respect.

## 3. Explain where the signal comes from

- Introduce the key differentiator: you can forward peer newsletters into Digest Engine using a dedicated intake address.
- Explain that Digest Engine parses those newsletters and looks at who is being cited, linked to, and mentioned repeatedly.
- Make the point that this is valuable because peer newsletters reflect actual editorial judgment, not algorithmic hype.
- Avoid implementation detail beyond the user-facing idea that repeated trusted mentions strengthen authority over time.

## 4. Show what changes for the reader in day-to-day work

- Cover how authority-aware ranking helps surface quieter experts before they become mainstream.
- Explain how it helps readers avoid overweighting loud accounts that generate attention without offering substance.
- Show how it improves prioritization: when several strong sources keep pointing to the same person, company, or theme, that signal should affect what rises in the shortlist.
- Mention practical outcomes such as seeing that multiple trusted editors have covered a source recently, or using authority as an extra filter when reviewing content.

## 5. Give a concrete editorial scenario

- Walk through a simple example relevant to marketers or newsletter writers.
- Example shape: two stories arrive on the same day, one from a loud social account and one tied to a company several trusted niche newsletters have referenced lately.
- Show how authority-aware ranking helps the editor spend time on the second story first.
- End the scenario by tying it back to reader trust: better prioritization leads to better issues, not just a cleaner queue.

## 6. Explain why this matters specifically for marketers and newsletter authors

- Marketers need to spot which voices are shaping the conversation before everyone else repeats the same talking points.
- Newsletter authors need a better way to decide what deserves limited space in an issue.
- Emphasize that the feature supports stronger editorial judgment, not automated decision-making.
- Position Digest Engine as a tool that helps readers make sharper calls faster.

## 7. Address the likely objection

- Question to address: "Does this just reinforce the same established names?"
- Answer: no, because the goal is not to freeze the ranking around famous people, but to notice who is being endorsed by trusted editors right now.
- Mention that authority can shift over time, which helps rising voices surface when credible sources begin paying attention to them.
- Keep this section short and reassuring.

## 8. Close with the practical takeaway

- Re-state the core benefit: authority-aware ranking helps readers find signal earlier and with more confidence.
- Tie back to the editorial job: less time spent sorting hype, more time spent choosing stories that actually matter.
- End with a forward-looking bridge to adjacent features, such as unified entity profiles or competitive intelligence.
