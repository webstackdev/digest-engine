# Draft assembly, not just curation

## Working angle

- Core promise: show that Digest Engine helps readers get from research to a working draft faster, without taking authorship away from them.
- Audience lens: marketers and newsletter authors who know the hardest part is often starting, structuring, and turning notes into a coherent issue.
- Tone: practical, editorial, and reassuring. The product helps you begin writing; it does not claim to replace writing.
- Suggested headline direction: *From ranked shortlist to outlined draft.*

## 1. Open with the blank-page problem

- Start with the most familiar frustration: even after a good week of curation, the hardest moment can still be opening a blank draft and figuring out how the issue should flow.
- Frame the hidden cost: research can be done, but the issue is still not close to publish-ready.
- Make the contrast clear: most tools stop at curation, leaving the writer to build the issue structure alone.

## 2. Define what Digest Engine does in plain language

- Explain that Digest Engine does not just gather strong source material. It helps assemble that material into a draft outline.
- Clarify that the system groups related items into likely sections, carries forward useful summaries, and gives the writer a head start on structure.
- Emphasize the positioning: the goal is to remove blank-page friction, not to turn the writer into an editor of machine output.

## 3. Explain where the draft comes from

- Introduce the inputs in simple terms: high-relevance recent content, promoted themes, and original content ideas.
- Explain that the draft reflects the curation work the reader has already done, rather than inventing a disconnected newsletter from scratch.
- Make the connection to earlier features: ranking, theme detection, and shortlist review all feed into the draft stage.

## 4. Show what the first draft actually looks like

- Describe the output in user-facing terms: a proposed issue structure with sections, supporting items, and summaries already attached.
- Mention that themes can become draft sections, which helps the issue feel organized rather than like a pile of links.
- Explain that this gives the writer something concrete to react to: reorder, combine, cut, expand, or rewrite.

## 5. Make the writer-control point explicit

- State clearly that Digest Engine is not trying to automate the writer out of the process.
- Explain that the writer stays in charge of framing, transitions, emphasis, and final voice.
- Mention useful controls such as reordering sections, rewriting passages, regenerating a section, or exporting the draft into the tools they already use.
- This section should directly answer the fear that "AI draft generation" means generic writing.

## 6. Give a concrete scenario

- Walk through a realistic example for a newsletter author.
- Example shape: the editor approves a few strong articles, promotes one or two themes, and gets a draft with a clear opening section, supporting items, and a closing idea.
- Show how this changes the job from "What do I even write first?" to "Which of these sections should lead, and how do I sharpen the voice?"
- Tie the example back to time saved and better issue momentum.

## 7. Explain why this matters for marketers and newsletter authors

- Marketers often need to turn scattered source material into a coherent point of view quickly.
- Newsletter authors need a repeatable way to move from collection to composition without every issue feeling like a cold start.
- Emphasize that draft assembly helps readers ship more consistently because the product supports the hardest transition in the workflow.

## 8. Address the likely objection

- Question to address: "Won't this make every newsletter sound the same?"
- Answer: no, because the system is assembling from the sources, themes, and feedback inside one project, and the human writer still shapes the final piece.
- Reassure the reader that the point is not to standardize voice, but to reduce startup friction.
- Keep this section concise and confident.

## 9. Connect it to ongoing iteration

- Explain that the first draft is not the end of the process.
- Mention that section-level edits, regeneration, and feedback help the system become more useful over time.
- Show that the draft is a living working document rather than a one-shot output.

## 10. Close with the takeaway

- Re-state the practical benefit: Digest Engine helps turn a curated shortlist into a usable issue draft faster.
- Tie this back to the writer’s real goal: spending more time refining the newsletter and less time fighting the starting point.
- End with a bridge to related features like composable AI skills or relevance training.
