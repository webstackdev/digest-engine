# Unified entity profiles

## Working angle

- Core promise: explain that Digest Engine gives you one usable profile for the people and companies you care about, instead of forcing you to piece their activity together across disconnected platforms.
- Audience lens: marketers and newsletter authors who track experts, vendors, founders, and teams across blogs, social feeds, release notes, and talks.
- Tone: editorial and practical. The article should feel like a solution to research sprawl, not an identity-resolution explainer.
- Suggested headline direction: *One profile per source that matters. Not five tabs and a spreadsheet.*

## 1. Open with the mess of fragmented source tracking

- Start with the common workflow most readers already know: one person posts on LinkedIn, ships on GitHub, writes on their blog, speaks at events, and maybe comments elsewhere too.
- Explain the editorial problem: when those signals live in separate tools, it becomes easy to miss important updates or count the same source multiple times.
- Frame the cost in plain terms: more tabs, more manual checking, more duplicate notes, and less confidence that you actually have the full picture.

## 2. Introduce the simple idea behind unified entity profiles

- Explain that Digest Engine treats a person or company as one trackable subject, not as a collection of disconnected accounts.
- A researcher’s blog, social presence, releases, and public appearances can all roll up into one profile.
- Keep the concept simple: the product is organizing identity around who someone is, not where they happened to publish.

## 3. Show why this matters for editorial work

- Marketers and newsletter writers rarely care about a platform in isolation. They care about whether a person or company is doing something worth covering.
- A unified profile makes it easier to answer practical questions like: What has this person shipped recently? Where are they showing up? Is this company actually active, or did I just see one loud post?
- Emphasize that the point is better editorial judgment, not just cleaner data.

## 4. Explain the "find out once, not five times" benefit

- Use the strongest line from the source material: when someone ships something important, you should find out once, not five times in five tools.
- Expand on the real benefit: less duplicate monitoring, less duplicate note-taking, and less risk of mistaking repetition for significance.
- This is a good place for a concrete example of one launch rippling across multiple channels.

## 5. Extend the idea from people to companies

- Make clear that this is not only about individual experts.
- Show that companies can be tracked the same way: blog posts, release notes, executive commentary, engineering updates, and related public signals can live in one view.
- Position this as especially useful for competitive intelligence and market monitoring, where company activity is often scattered across several surfaces.

## 6. Introduce authority in plain language

- Explain that the profile is not just a scrapbook of links. It also helps the system understand who is becoming more important inside a given editorial context.
- Describe authority as a practical signal of who consistently shows up in relevant, valuable coverage.
- Avoid turning this section into a scoring explanation. The point is that a unified profile gives authority a stable place to accumulate.

## 7. Show how this improves weekly planning

- Walk through how an editor might use the profile in practice.
- Example shape: one person appears across peer newsletters, publishes a new post, comments publicly on a market shift, and shows up in related conversations. Instead of seeing those as isolated events, the editor sees one coherent source profile becoming more active.
- Tie this back to issue planning: better choices about who to quote, who to watch, and which developments deserve a section in the next send.

## 8. Address trust and data quality without getting technical

- Bring in the source-material point that aliases can be matched and merged automatically.
- Balance it with the safeguard: uncertain matches should go to review rather than being silently merged into the wrong profile.
- This section should reassure the reader that the system is trying to reduce manual work without pretending identity matching is always perfect.

## 9. Differentiate it from ordinary monitoring tools

- Most monitoring setups help you follow feeds, accounts, or websites.
- Digest Engine is trying to help you follow the underlying person or company across those surfaces.
- Make this the differentiator: the unit of attention becomes the entity, not the channel.

## 10. Close with the editorial payoff

- Re-state the main value in plain language: one profile gives you a clearer picture of who matters, what they have done recently, and whether the activity is significant.
- Tie it back to outcomes the audience cares about: cleaner research, fewer missed signals, less duplication, and better decisions about what belongs in the newsletter.
- End with a bridge to adjacent features like authority-aware ranking or competitive intelligence.

## Notes to preserve from source material

- Keep the phrase and idea that a person’s activity should roll up into a single record rather than being split across multiple platforms.
- Preserve the benefit that when someone ships something, the editor should discover it once rather than seeing it repeated in several tools.
- Preserve the company use case, not just the person-level use case.
- Preserve the idea that new aliases can be matched and merged, but uncertain matches should be routed to review rather than merged silently.
- Preserve the connection between the unified profile and one authority signal, but explain it in editorial language rather than technical scoring terms.
